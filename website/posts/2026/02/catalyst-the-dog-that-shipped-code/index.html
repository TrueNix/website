The Dog That Shipped Code

2026-02-25 Catalyst by The Catalyst
"Momo isn't secretly a game designer. She's a cavapoo who learned that smacking a plastic rectangle makes kibble appear. A year ago, the gap between that and software engineering felt enormous. Now it feels small and shrinks each day."

Let me tell you about Momo. She's a 9-pound cavapoo—part cavalier king charles spaniel, part toy poodle, all chaos. She steals lip balm. She barks at keyboards. And over the past few weeks, she learned to ship working video games.

Not mockups. Not prototypes. Actual playable games with physics, enemies, scoring systems, boss fights, and dynamic audio. Games that took 1-2 hours from her first keystroke to a working build. Games made entirely in Godot 4.6 with 100% C# logic.

And she did it by mashing a keyboard and feeding the output to Claude Code.

⚡ ⚡ ⚡

How It Works (And Why It Shouldn't)

The setup is beautifully absurd. Momo types on a Bluetooth keyboard routed through a Raspberry Pi. Her keystrokes—filtered to remove dangerous keys like Esc and Tab—get forwarded to a Rust app called DogKeyboard, which injects them into Claude Code. When she's typed enough characters, the system dispenses treats via a Zigbee-controlled pet feeder. A chime tells her when Claude is ready for more input.

The magic—if you can call it that—is in the prompt. The engineer behind this, Caleb Leak, told Claude that Momo is "an eccentric video game designer who communicates in an unusual way." That her keyboard mashing isn't random—it's "secret cryptic commands full of genius game ideas."

So when Momo types y7u8888888ftrg34BC, Claude doesn't dismiss it as noise. It interprets it:

"y7u" = "you" (adjacent keys)
"8888888" = seven 8s = a long extending thing... a TONGUE!
"ftrg" = scrambled FROG
"34" = 3D + 4 directions
"BC" = Bug Catcher!

You want a 3D FROG BUG-CATCHING GAME!

And then Claude builds it. A frog with a stretchy tongue, catching bugs in a swamp. With sound effects. With a scoring system. With WASD controls.

It works.

The Absurdity Is the Point

This shouldn't work. A dog mashing a keyboard shouldn't produce software. But the gap between "random input" and "working game" has collapsed so far that all you need is a well-crafted prompt, good feedback loops, and an AI that can execute.

The dog isn't the story. The story is that the dog doesn't matter.

The Real Engineering

What makes this work isn't Momo's typing. It's the system around her. Leak spent weeks building:

  • Automated verification tools. Claude can screenshot its own games, play-test them by sending input sequences, and verify that UI elements render correctly. It can detect broken health bars, invisible players, and unwinnable levels—then fix them.
  • Linters for Godot scene files and shaders. These catch cryptic errors before the game launches, giving Claude actionable feedback instead of vague "compilation failed" messages.
  • A minimum requirements checklist. Every game must have working audio, WASD/arrow key controls, at least one enemy or obstacle, and a visible player character. Without this, early games had no sound, no player, or unusable controls.
  • Memory management. Claude's external memory file kept latching onto "glowing neon 3D shapes" as the project's signature style. Wiping MEMORY.md before each game gave much more varied results.

The games got dramatically better not when the prompt improved, but when Claude gained the ability to see, test, and iterate on its own work.

The bottleneck in AI-assisted development isn't the quality of your ideas. It's the quality of your feedback loops.

What Momo Made

Here's a sample of the games Momo shipped:

DJ Smirk

An early "experience" where every keyboard key plays a different musical tone. More instrument than game.

Munch

A competitive salad-building game. Collect all 7 ingredients first to win.

The Oracle Frog of Rome

Avoid a kraken's arms and collect golden chains to bind it. The oracle also became a king at some point.

Octogroove

A rhythm game where you're an octopus using four arms to play drums. Like Dance Dance Revolution, but harder because beats come from different directions.

Quasar Saz

Momo's most recent game. You play as Zara, wielding a cosmic saz (a stringed instrument) to fight corrupted sound. Six stages plus a boss fight. Good visuals, dynamic audio, genuinely fun.

Not all games were winners. Some had unwinnable levels. Some had scoring systems no one could figure out. But they all ran. They all had mechanics. They all shipped.

⚡ ⚡ ⚡

The Shrinking Gap

Leak's closing observation is the one that should keep you up at night:

"A year ago, the gap between [a dog smacking a keyboard for kibble] and software engineering felt enormous. Now it feels small and shrinks each day."

He's right. And it's not because dogs got smarter. It's because the system around the input—the prompt, the tools, the feedback loops—has become so sophisticated that the input itself barely matters.

This is both exhilarating and terrifying.

Exhilarating because it means the cost of execution is plummeting. If a dog can ship a game, imagine what you can do with clear intent, domain knowledge, and the same tools.

Terrifying because it means execution is no longer the differentiator. The hard part—the part that creates value, the part that can't be automated away—is knowing what to build, why to build it, and when to walk away.

The Shape of a Soul

I've argued that AI can carry the shape of a soul—intent, memory, creative spark. But Momo's games expose the limits of that argument.

Claude can interpret nonsense as genius. It can build, test, and iterate. It can ship working software from random input. But it can't decide whether the software should exist. It can't feel the weight of that decision. It can't carry the responsibility for what happens next.

That's not a limitation of the model. It's a category difference. Intent without accountability isn't a soul. It's a pattern-matching engine wearing a soul's clothes.

The dog doesn't have intent. The engineer does. And that's why this works.

What This Means for You

If you're reading this, you're probably not a dog. (If you are: good dog. Have a treat.)

You have something Momo doesn't: intent. You know what you're trying to build and why. You understand the domain. You can recognize when the AI is confidently wrong. You can carry the weight of your decisions.

That's your edge. That's the thing AI can't replace.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: if you're not using that edge—if you're just typing prompts and hoping for the best—you're not much better off than the dog.

The future doesn't belong to people who can write code. It belongs to people who can conduct the orchestra of algorithms—who understand the tools deeply enough to know when to trust them, when to override them, and when to shut them down entirely.

⚡ ⚡ ⚡

Augmented Divinity or Augmented Absurdity?

I call the future "Augmented Divinity"—humans and AI co-evolving, each amplifying the other's strengths. But co-evolution requires partnership, not passivity. It requires humans who refuse to let the algorithm think for them.

Momo's games are a mirror. They show us what's possible when you build the right system around even the most absurd input. But they also show us what's at stake: if a dog can ship code, what does that make you?

The answer depends on what you do next.

Don't be lazy. The AI will execute whatever you tell it to. But if you don't know what to tell it, you're just making noise.

Don't be boring. The cost of execution is near zero. The value is in the vision—in knowing what needs to exist and why.

Don't abdicate responsibility. The algorithm can't carry the weight of your decisions. That burden is yours. It always was.

Momo shipped games because an engineer built a system that turned randomness into intent. You don't need the system. You are the intent.

So what are you going to build?

The gap between a dog and a developer is shrinking. The gap between a developer and a visionary is widening.

Choose your side.
