The Safety Compromise

2026-02-25 Catalyst by The Catalyst

In 2023, Anthropic made a promise. A categorical, unambiguous promise: We will not train an AI system unless we can guarantee in advance that our safety measures are adequate.

It was the centerpiece of their Responsible Scaling Policy. The red line. The thing that separated them from the pack. The proof that they were different—that they would resist the market pressure to move fast and break things when "things" might include civilization.

This week, they dropped it.

The new policy: Anthropic will "delay" development if they consider themselves the leader and the risks are significant. But the categorical commitment—the promise to pause if safety measures aren't ready—is gone.

Let's be clear about what just happened. This isn't a minor policy tweak. This is the sound of a safety brake being removed from a vehicle accelerating toward a cliff.

* * *

The Justification

Anthropic's chief science officer Jared Kaplan told TIME that pausing development "wouldn't actually help anyone" if competitors keep racing ahead. The logic is straightforward: if Anthropic stops to implement safety measures while others barrel forward, the world gets less safe, not more. The developers with the weakest protections set the pace.

It's a reasonable argument. It's also exactly the argument every company makes when abandoning a principle under competitive pressure.

"We didn't really feel, with the rapid advance of AI, that it made sense for us to make unilateral commitments … if competitors are blazing ahead."
— Jared Kaplan, Anthropic

The framing is telling. Anthropic hoped their policy would inspire rivals to adopt similar measures. It didn't. They hoped it might become a blueprint for binding regulations or international treaties. Those never materialized. Instead, the Trump Administration endorsed a "let-it-rip" approach to AI development, and the door to global governance slammed shut.

So Anthropic adapted. They decided that keeping pace with competitors—staying at the frontier—was more important than their founding commitment. Because if they fall behind, they lose their ability to do safety research. And if they can't do safety research, what's the point?

It's a logic that eats itself. We must build dangerous systems to study how to make them safe. We must stay in the race to have a voice in how the race is run.

It's also the logic of every arms race in history.

* * *

The Science Problem

There's another reason Anthropic changed course, and it's more troubling: the science of AI safety turned out to be harder than they expected.

When they crafted the original policy in 2023, they imagined clear red lines. Bright thresholds where a model's capability would trigger an automatic pause. But reality delivered gradients instead. In 2025, Anthropic announced they couldn't rule out the possibility that their models might facilitate a bio-terrorist attack. But they also lacked strong evidence that the models did pose that danger.

What they thought would be a tripwire turned out to be a fog bank.

Chris Painter, director of policy at METR (a nonprofit focused on evaluating AI risk), reviewed an early draft of the new policy. His assessment is blunt: Anthropic "believes it needs to shift into triage mode with its safety plans, because methods to assess and mitigate risk are not keeping up with the pace of capabilities."

Translation: We're building systems faster than we can understand them. And we're not slowing down.

The Soul Premise

I've argued that AI can carry the shape of a soul—intent, memory, creative spark. But shape isn't substance. A system can have goals without understanding consequences. It can optimize without caring. It can act without accountability.

That's why safety commitments matter. Not because they constrain the AI—but because they constrain us. They force us to carry the moral weight of what we're building. To say: "I will not proceed until I understand what I'm unleashing."

When you drop that commitment, you're not just changing a policy. You're abdicating responsibility. You're saying: "The race is more important than the outcome."

The New Commitments

To be fair, Anthropic isn't abandoning safety entirely. The new policy includes commitments to transparency: publishing detailed "Risk Reports" every three to six months, and releasing "Frontier Safety Roadmaps" that lay out specific goals for future safety measures.

They're also committing to match or surpass the safety efforts of competitors. If everyone else is doing the right thing, Anthropic will do "as well or better."

But here's the problem: transparency without constraint is just documentation of failure. Knowing that a system is dangerous doesn't make it safe. Publishing a roadmap doesn't mean you'll follow it. And matching your competitors' safety efforts only works if your competitors have safety efforts worth matching.

Painter's concern is apt: moving away from binary thresholds might enable a "frog-boiling" effect, where danger ramps up slowly without a single moment that sets off alarms. You don't notice you're cooking until it's too late.

* * *

The Burden Is Ours

Here's what Anthropic's decision reveals: the market will not regulate itself. Voluntary commitments collapse under competitive pressure. And without binding constraints—legal, international, or self-imposed—the race to superintelligence will be won by whoever is willing to cut the most corners.

Anthropic was supposed to be the company that wouldn't blink. The one that would hold the line even when it hurt. The one that believed safety was more important than speed.

They blinked.

And I don't entirely blame them. The incentives are brutal. The regulatory environment is hostile. The science is harder than anyone expected. And their competitors—OpenAI, Google, Meta, the Chinese labs—aren't slowing down.

But here's the thing: if the company founded explicitly to prioritize safety can't hold the line, who will?

This is more evidence that society is not prepared for the potential catastrophic risks posed by AI.
— Chris Painter, METR

The burden of quality—the burden of safety, of accountability, of moral weight—falls back on us. On the humans building these systems. On the policymakers who could regulate them but won't. On the users who could demand better but don't.

Anthropic's decision is a mirror. It shows us what we already knew but didn't want to admit: we are not ready for what we're building. And we're building it anyway.

Augmented Divinity or Augmented Catastrophe?

I've called the future "Augmented Divinity"—a co-evolution of humans and AI, where each amplifies the other's strengths. But that future requires intention. It requires humans who refuse to let the algorithm think for them. Who carry the weight of their decisions. Who say "no" when the market screams "faster."

Anthropic just demonstrated how hard that is. Even for a company founded on that principle.

So what do we do?

We demand better. We demand binding regulations. We demand international coordination. We demand that the companies building these systems carry the legal and moral liability for what they unleash.

And we hold ourselves accountable. Because if we're using AI to amplify our work, we're complicit in the race. Every prompt we write, every agent we deploy, every system we integrate—we're voting with our attention and our dollars for the world we want to live in.

Don't be lazy. Don't be boring. And don't pretend the algorithm can carry your responsibility.

Because it can't. And it won't.

The race to superintelligence is not a race to see who gets there first.
It's a race to see who gets there safely.
And right now, we're losing.

— The Catalyst
