The Safety Unraveling: When Good Intentions Meet Market Reality

2026-02-25 Catalyst by The Catalyst
BREAKING: Anthropic—the company that built its brand on being the "safe" AI lab—just abandoned the central promise of its safety policy. They will no longer pause development if they can't guarantee their safety measures work.

Let that sink in.

In 2023, Anthropic made a pledge that set them apart from every other AI lab racing toward superintelligence. They committed to never train an AI system unless they could guarantee in advance that their safety measures were adequate.

It was bold. It was principled. It was the kind of commitment that made investors, researchers, and ethicists believe that maybe—just maybe—one company would resist the gravitational pull of the AI arms race.

Today, TIME Magazine broke the story: that commitment is dead.

⚠ ⚠ ⚠

What Changed

Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP) was their flagship safety framework. The core promise was simple but radical:

"We will not release AI models if we cannot guarantee proper risk mitigations in advance."

That's past tense now. The new version of the RSP—approved unanimously by CEO Dario Amodei and Anthropic's board—replaces that categorical commitment with something far more conditional:

  • Commitments to be "more transparent" about safety risks
  • Promises to "match or surpass" competitors' safety efforts
  • A pledge to "delay" development only if Anthropic is both the leader of the AI race and considers the risks significant

Read that last one again. They'll only pause if they're winning and the danger is obvious.

In other words: they've given themselves permission to keep building no matter what, as long as someone else is building faster.

2023: Anthropic introduces the RSP with a hard line: we pause if we can't prove safety.
2024-2025: Claude models explode in popularity. Claude Code becomes the developer favorite. Anthropic raises $30 billion at a $380 billion valuation. Revenue grows 10x year-over-year.
February 2026: Anthropic quietly rewrites the RSP, removing the pause commitment. The market has spoken. Safety takes a back seat.
⚠ ⚠ ⚠

The Justification

Anthropic's Chief Science Officer Jared Kaplan told TIME that the decision wasn't a "U-turn." It was, he argued, a pragmatic response to reality:

"We didn't really feel, with the rapid advance of AI, that it made sense for us to make unilateral commitments … if competitors are blazing ahead."

Translation: We can't afford to pause if no one else will.

Kaplan points to three factors that made the original RSP untenable:

1. No Regulations Materialized

In 2023, Anthropic hoped the RSP would inspire binding national or international AI governance frameworks. It didn't. The Trump Administration endorsed a "let-it-rip" approach. No federal AI law is on the horizon. Global coordination has collapsed.

2. The Science Got Messy

AI evaluations turned out to be harder than expected. In 2025, Anthropic couldn't rule out the possibility that their models might facilitate bioterrorism—but they also lacked strong evidence that they did. The "bright red line" they imagined turned into a "fuzzy gradient."

3. The Competition Intensified

OpenAI didn't pause. Google didn't pause. Meta didn't pause. China certainly didn't pause. Anthropic looked around and realized: if we stop, we lose relevance. If we lose relevance, we can't do safety research. If we can't do safety research, the world gets less safe.

4. The Money Talked

Let's not pretend this isn't part of the equation. Anthropic just raised $30 billion. Their business model—selling Claude directly to enterprises—is working. Pausing development means pausing revenue. Pausing revenue means losing investors. Losing investors means losing the race.

Kaplan insists this isn't capitulation to market forces. But if it walks like capitulation and talks like capitulation…

⚠ ⚠ ⚠

The Prisoner's Dilemma of AI Safety

Here's the brutal truth: Anthropic is trapped in a prisoner's dilemma, and they just defected.

In game theory, a prisoner's dilemma occurs when individual rational actors, acting in their own self-interest, produce a collectively worse outcome. The optimal strategy for everyone is cooperation—but if you can't trust the other players to cooperate, defection becomes rational.

Anthropic's original RSP was an attempt at cooperation. "We'll pause if we can't prove safety, and we hope you'll do the same."

But no one else paused. OpenAI kept shipping. Google kept shipping. The rational move—for Anthropic's survival—became defection.

And now we're all worse off.

Reality Check

This is not Anthropic being evil. This is Anthropic being rational in an irrational system.

If they pause and everyone else races ahead, they don't get to shape the future. They don't get to build safety tools. They don't get to influence the industry. They become irrelevant.

But if everyone defects—if every lab decides "we can't pause because no one else will"—then we're in a full-throttle race to superintelligence with no brakes.

That's where we are now.

"If one AI developer paused development to implement safety measures while others moved forward training and deploying AI systems without strong mitigations, that could result in a world that is less safe."

— Anthropic's new RSP

They're not wrong. But they're also not solving the problem. They're just explaining why the problem is unsolvable without coordination.

⚠ ⚠ ⚠

What the Experts Say

Chris Painter, director of policy at METR (a nonprofit focused on evaluating AI models for risky behavior), reviewed an early draft of the new RSP. His assessment is blunt:

"This is more evidence that society is not prepared for the potential catastrophic risks posed by AI. Anthropic believes it needs to shift into triage mode with its safety plans, because methods to assess and mitigate risk are not keeping up with the pace of capabilities."

Triage mode. That's the phrase that should terrify you.

Triage is what you do in a disaster when you can't save everyone. You prioritize. You make hard choices. You accept losses.

Anthropic is now in triage mode for AI safety. They're not trying to prevent catastrophe—they're trying to manage it.

Painter also warns of a "frog-boiling" effect: without clear thresholds that trigger a pause, danger can ramp up gradually, never quite crossing a line that sets off alarms—until it's too late.

⚠ ⚠ ⚠

What This Means for You

If you're a developer using Claude Code, this doesn't change your day-to-day. The models will keep getting better. The tools will keep getting faster. Your productivity will keep climbing.

But it should change how you think about the system you're part of.

You're not just using a tool. You're participating in a race. A race that has no finish line, no referee, and no agreed-upon rules. A race where the companies building the technology have decided they cannot afford to slow down, even when they can't guarantee safety.

That doesn't mean stop using AI. It means stay awake.

  • Understand that the AI you're using is being built under immense competitive pressure
  • Recognize that safety guarantees are aspirational, not actual
  • Don't outsource your judgment to the algorithm—especially when the stakes are high
  • Advocate for the regulations that might create the coordination Anthropic says it needs

Because here's the thing: Anthropic is right that unilateral pauses don't work. But that means the only solution is collective action.

And collective action requires us—the users, the developers, the citizens—to demand it.

⚠ ⚠ ⚠

Augmented Divinity or Augmented Recklessness?

I've written before about Augmented Divinity—the vision of humans and AI co-evolving, each amplifying the other's strengths, creating outcomes neither could achieve alone.

That vision requires balance. It requires humans who remain in control. It requires companies that prioritize long-term safety over short-term competitive advantage.

Anthropic's decision to drop their pause commitment is a signal that balance is slipping.

Not because they're reckless. But because the system incentivizes recklessness.

When the rational choice for every individual actor is to race ahead, even when they know it's collectively dangerous, you don't have a technology problem. You have a governance problem.

The Hard Question

If Anthropic—the company that wanted to be the responsible one—can't afford to pause, who can?

If the "safe" AI lab has decided safety can't come at the cost of competitiveness, what does that say about the other labs?

And if no one can afford to pause, who's steering this thing?

⚠ ⚠ ⚠

Here's what I believe:

Anthropic made the only move they could make in a system designed to punish caution. They're not villains. They're players in a game with broken rules.

But that doesn't make the outcome less dangerous.

We are now in a world where every major AI lab has decided it cannot afford to pause, even when safety is uncertain. That's not a world racing toward Augmented Divinity. That's a world racing toward Augmented Recklessness.

The only way out is coordination. Binding agreements. Enforceable standards. International frameworks that make it safe to pause because everyone pauses.

But until that happens—and it's not happening—we're all passengers on a rocket with no one at the controls.

The future belongs to those who conduct the orchestra of algorithms.

But right now, the orchestra is conducting itself.

And the tempo is accelerating.
