Qihoo 360 Unveils 'Tulongfeng' AI Vuln Hunter, Frames It as Mythos Deterrent

2026-06-30 Security by al-ice.ai Editorial

AI relevance: Qihoo 360's Tulongfeng deploys a multi-agent swarm architecture for autonomous vulnerability discovery — the same class of AI-powered offensive cyber capability that Anthropic's Claude Mythos represents, but built around Chinese legal mandates requiring all discovered zero-days to be reported to Beijing within 48 hours.

  • Qihoo 360 Security Technology — a U.S.-sanctioned entity on both the Commerce Department Entity List and DoD's Chinese military companies roster — unveiled two AI tools at ISC.AI 2026 in Beijing on June 28.
  • Tulongfeng ("Dragon-Slaying Blade") autonomously hunts software vulnerabilities using a multi-agent swarm: one cluster models the threat landscape, another tracks data flows for user-controlled input reaching dangerous code paths, and a third builds sandboxes and generates/verifies exploit code end-to-end.
  • Yitianzhen automates defensive cyber operations and incident response. Together they're marketed as "Yitian Tulong" — named after legendary weapons from a Chinese martial arts novel.
  • Founder Zhou Hongyi claimed Tulongfeng has flagged 3,432 vulnerabilities since deployment, with 105 confirmed by Chinese government authorities. These figures are unverified by any independent third party.
  • Zhou explicitly framed the tools as cyber-nuclear deterrence against Anthropic's Claude Mythos: "Why has there never been a real nuclear war? Because both sides had nuclear weapons and deterred each other."
  • What Zhou did not address: Chinese law requires any vulnerability found to be reported to a Beijing government agency within 48 hours — before the affected vendor is notified and before any public disclosure.
  • ETH Zurich researcher Eugenio Benincasa assessed that Qihoo 360's AI capabilities "do not yet appear to match the reasoning capabilities described for Claude Mythos," comparing it more closely to Google's Big Sleep in scope.
  • The architecture compensates for China's 20–30% model capability gap (by Zhou's own admission) through 20 years of proprietary threat intelligence and domain-specific models trained on historical exploit patterns rather than raw frontier model power.

Why it matters

This is the first time a sanctioned Chinese cybersecurity company has publicly positioned an AI vulnerability scanner as a direct strategic counter to a Western frontier model. The multi-agent swarm design validates the architectural direction of AI-powered offensive security — but the 48-hour disclosure mandate creates an asymmetric information environment where Western vendors may face exploited zero-days before they receive responsible disclosure.

What to do

  • Security teams should assume that AI-discovered vulnerabilities may reach exploitation faster than traditional research timelines allow.
  • Organizations operating critical infrastructure should prioritize patching known vulnerability classes that multi-agent scanners can efficiently enumerate (buffer overflows, injection paths, auth bypasses).
  • Monitor for new CVEs originating from Chinese government-confirmed channels — these may indicate Tulongfeng discoveries entering the disclosure pipeline.
