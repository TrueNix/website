Reuters — NSA reportedly uses Anthropic's Claude Mythos despite government blacklist

2026-04-20 Security by al-ice.ai Editorial

AI relevance: A US intelligence agency is reportedly deploying an autonomous vulnerability-discovery AI model for offensive and defensive cyber operations — directly contradicting the federal government's own ban on the vendor, raising questions about how AI procurement policy adapts when capability outpaces regulation.

What happened

  • Reuters reported on April 19 that the NSA is reportedly using Anthropic's Claude Mythos model, despite the Trump administration's executive order banning federal agencies from doing business with Anthropic and the Department of Defense labeling the company a "supply-chain risk."
  • According to Axios, the NSA is among the roughly 40 organizations that have access to Mythos through Anthropic's Project Glasswing — though Anthropic has not publicly named the participating entities.
  • Mythos is Anthropic's most capable model for coding and agentic tasks, with demonstrated ability to autonomously detect, chain, and exploit software vulnerabilities — capabilities that led Anthropic to restrict its release to a limited partner program rather than a public launch.
  • White House officials are reportedly re-evaluating the Anthropic ban as Mythos' potential for identifying vulnerabilities in government infrastructure becomes clear, suggesting a policy reversal may be underway.
  • The contradiction highlights a growing tension: the US government banned Anthropic over policy disagreements while its own intelligence agencies are actively using the company's most powerful cybersecurity tool.

Why it matters

This is a concrete example of AI capability outpacing government procurement policy. When a model is powerful enough to autonomously discover and chain zero-day vulnerabilities across every major OS and browser, the question of who gets access becomes a national security issue — not just a corporate one. The fact that an intelligence agency is reportedly using the model despite an active federal ban suggests that operational need is already overriding political decisions, which has implications for:

  • AI governance frameworks: Executive orders banning AI vendors may be unenforceable against intelligence agencies with classified procurement authorities.
  • Supply-chain risk assessment: The DoD's "supply-chain risk" designation for Anthropic is now at odds with NSA operational use, creating conflicting guidance for defense contractors.
  • Offensive/defensive asymmetry: If the NSA has access to Mythos-level capabilities and adversaries can access similar models through underground channels (as Suzu Labs reported this week), the offensive AI gap may narrow faster than policy can address.

What to do

  • Organizations in government supply chains should monitor for conflicting guidance between DoD risk designations and intelligence agency procurement — compliance requirements may shift rapidly.
  • Security teams should assume that nation-state actors have access to autonomous vulnerability-discovery AI and adjust patch timelines accordingly.
  • Track Project Glasswing participant announcements — the composition of this 40-organization group will signal which sectors Anthropic considers highest-priority for AI-powered cyber defense.
